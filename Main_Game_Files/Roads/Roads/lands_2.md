A global sea-level drop of -2,000 meters fundamentally rewires Earth’s climate, geography, and ecosystems. The planet's surface area expands dramatically as ocean basins dry out, creating a stark, vertical division between the old world (the new high-altitude plateaus and alpine mountains) and the new world (the hyper-dense, pressurized abyssal lowlands).Here is how this drastic shift integrates into the global design, including the global climate impacts, radical texture differences, and the reality of "underwater" world living.1. The Global Shift: How the Planet ChangesWith the oceans retreated into the deepest trenches (like the Mariana, Puerto Rico, and Cayman trenches), the planet's water volume is severely restricted.Atmospheric Crushing: Air behaves like water—it settles into the lowest points. The old continents (North America, Europe, Africa) are now massive, arid, thin-aired alpine plateaus sitting 2,000 meters above the new baseline. Conversely, the newly exposed ocean floors experience intense atmospheric pressure ($~1.2$ to $1.5 \text{ atm}$ or more), trapping heat and creating a hyper-oxygenated greenhouse effect in the deep basins.The New Global Weather Grid: Rain is rare on the old continents because moisture from the deep, narrow trench-seas struggles to rise past the massive 2,000-meter continental shelves. Weather is dictated by violent orographic lift—storms brew in the deep basins, smash against the vertical continental walls, and dump torrential rain only along the cliff faces, leaving the high plateaus desert-dry and the abyssal floors swampy and humid.2. Terrain & Texture DifferencesThe visual and physical texture of the world is split into distinct vertical tiers, shifting drastically as you descend from the old world to the deep floor.Elevation ZoneTerrain TypeTexture & Visual ProfileThe High Plateaus(Old Continents: +2,000m)Alpine Tundra & Cold DesertsWeathered, wind-scoured bedrock, gray granite, dust storms, and sparse, hardy pine-shrub vegetation.The Shelf Edge(Old Coastlines: 0m to -200m)The Fossilized Reef BeltRazor-sharp, jagged calcified coral forests; jagged limestone cliffs, massive salt-crusted shipwrecks, and petrified whale skeletons turned into calcified micro-habitats.The Abyssal Basins(The New Plains: -200m to -2,000m)Hyper-Dense Wetlands & Silt FlatsThick, fine-grain gray and black abyssal silt that turns into treacherous quicksand-like mud when wet; vast, blinding white salt flats; and dense, fleshy, bioluminescent flora adapted to heavy air.3. "Underwater" World Living (Abyssal Civilization)Humanity and the synthetic "NetNavi" populations have migrated down into the deep basins because that is where the remaining water, heat, and atmospheric density are concentrated. Living in the former deep ocean requires adapting to a completely alien environment.The Sub-Oceanic Cities (Trench Metropolises)Cities are built directly into the vertical walls of the deepest trenches, descending downward toward the narrow, remaining hyper-saline seas.Vertical Architecture: Buildings don't expand outward; they hang. Metropolises are constructed as modular, multi-tiered steel structures anchored deep into the basalt volcanic rock of the trench walls via massive tension cables.Pressure-Harnessed Infrastructure: Because the air pressure is so high, heavy industry utilizes pneumatic power grids. Massive air-compressor stations at the top of the trenches channel the dense air downward through sprawling pipe networks to power automated manufacturing and transit systems without relying entirely on traditional electricity.Texture of Everyday Abyssal LifeBioluminescent Lighting: With the dense, humid cloud cover permanently blanketing the deep trenches, natural sunlight is filtered into a perpetual twilight. Cities and outposts are illuminated by harvesting the glowing, bio-engineered algae and fungi that evolved from deep-sea organisms, giving the lower world a neon, synthwave, or cyberpunk-like ambient glow.The "Mud-Crawler" Transit Grid: Standard wheels fail in the thick, heavy silt of the basin floors. Ground transportation relies on heavy, tracked vehicles or massive, low-pressure balloon tires designed to slide and grip across slick salt flats and wet abyssal clay. Highways are elevated, concrete-capped causeways built on top of ancient mid-ocean ridges.4. Environmental & Industrial IntegrationThe Brine HarvestWater is the ultimate currency. Settlements do not mine for gold; they mine for freshwater. Massive Siphon Spires sit on the edge of the trench seas, boiling the super-salty, mineral-rich water using local geothermal vents. The distilled fresh water is pumped up to the high-altitude plateaus through colossal pipeline networks, while the leftover crystalline salt and rare metals are scraped from the boiler plates to build structures and components.Corrosive AtmosphereThe combination of high humidity, dense oxygen, and airborne salt particles means that metal oxidizes at an accelerated rate. The dominant structural materials are non-metallic: advanced carbon-fiber composites, volcanic basalt glass, and treated coral-crete. Any exposed steel or copper quickly develops a thick, protective patina of green or rust, making older infrastructure look ancient, weathered, and organic.
