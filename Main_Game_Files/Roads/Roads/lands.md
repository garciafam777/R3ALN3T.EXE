1. New Regions & BiomesThe Abyssal Plains (The New Lowlands)Biome Atmosphere: This is the exposed sea floor, sitting at 0 to -2,000m relative to old sea levels. Because the air pressure is significantly higher here, the atmosphere is dense, heavy, and traps heat. It feels like a subtropical, hyper-oxygenated greenhouse.Landscape: The ground consists of vast, salt-encrusted flats, petrified coral forests that have adapted into hardened, stone-like flora, and deep, muddy chasms where the last remnants of saltwater have collected into hypersaline lakes.The Sky-Spine Range (The Cordillera Megalith)Biome Atmosphere: This is the old main island of Puerto Rico. Because it already stood up to 1,338 meters (Cerro de Punta) above the old sea level, it now reaches an absolute altitude of over 3,300 meters above the new ocean floor.Landscape: The climate here is alpine, crisp, and thin-aired. The lush tropical rainforests of the past (like El Yunque) have evolved into high-altitude cloud forests or cold, misty pine-shrub territories. The old northern Karst region is now a terrifying vertical wall of exposed limestone cliffs, riddled with massive cave networks that look down onto the plains below.The Trench BasinsBiome Atmosphere: To the north lies the Puerto Rico Trench. While the sea levels dropped 2,000m, this ultra-deep chasm still holds a narrow, incredibly deep, and highly volatile hyper-saline sea. The shores are shrouded in permanent sulfur mists and heavy cloud cover due to intense thermal evaporation.  2. Architectural Typologies & BuildingsAbyssal Siphon StationsPurpose: Water reclamation and mining.Design: Massive, industrial monolithic structures built directly over the lowest points of the old seabed. They utilize heavy pressure vacuums to draw brine from the deep salt lakes, distilling it into freshwater for the high-altitude cities while extracting rare earth minerals and lithium from the dried crust. They are industrial, metallic, and heavily shielded against salt corrosion.Pressure-Lock Habitations (Lowland Settlements)Purpose: Residential and commercial hubs for the workers on the new plains.Design: Because the atmospheric pressure at -2,000m is dense and thick, architecture here features dome-like structures, reinforced curved walls, and heavy ventilation towers. Buildings are constructed using local coral-crete—a composite material made from crushed, petrified seafloor coral and synthetic polymers.Cloud-Anchor Monasteries & Research SpiresPurpose: High-altitude observation, atmospheric manipulation, and elite residences.Design: Located on the highest peaks of the Sky-Spine (the old summits). These structures are built out of lightweight carbon-fiber composites and aerodynamic glass to withstand the fierce, high-altitude winds. They feature massive kinetic wind turbines and condensation nets that catch the clouds to harvest pristine rainwater.3. Statues & MonumentsThe Colossus of the Abyss (The Mariner’s Ghost)Location: Standing on a high cliffside that used to be the historic coast of San Juan, looking down into the dry basin.Description: A massive, 150-meter-tall statue forged from dark, oxidized copper and brass. It depicts a classic seafaring navigator holding an astrolabe, but instead of looking at the stars, the figure points downward into the vast desert plains below—a monument dedicated to the lost oceans and the civilization that once sailed them.The Pillars of the TrenchLocation: Bordering the dangerous northern highways that skirt the edge of the Puerto Rico Trench.Description: Giant, brutalist obelisks carved directly from the exposed volcanic bedrock. They serve both a symbolic purpose (marking the boundary of the known livable world) and a structural one, acting as massive anchors for the heavy gravitational and atmospheric monitoring equipment used to track tectonic shifts along the dry fault lines.4. Environmental Anomalies & InfrastructureThe Coral SpiresVisual Hazard: In the mid-elevation zones (the old coral reefs at -50m to -200m), the landscape is dominated by massive, towering skeletons of ancient barrier reefs. Over a thousand years, these have mineralized into razor-sharp, colorful crystalline structures. Roads cutting through this zone must be heavily shielded with reinforced concrete barrier walls to prevent vehicles from running into the jagged stone.Geothermal Thermal-VentsEnergy Grid: Deep on the old ocean floor, ancient volcanic fissures that were once deep-sea hydrothermal vents are now completely exposed to the air. The 3026 settlements have built heavy, geometric geothermal capture caps directly over these vents, channeling the raw subterranean heat into clean, endless energy for the transit grids and highways running across the basin.
